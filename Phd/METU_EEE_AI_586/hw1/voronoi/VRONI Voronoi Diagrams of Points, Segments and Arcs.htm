VRONI: Voronoi Diagrams of Points, Segments and Arcs in 2D



In a recent editorial, Fortune wrote that "it is notoriously difficult to obtain a practical implementation of an abstractly described geometric algorithm". According to my personal experience this remark is particularly true for the implementation of Voronoi diagrams (VDs) of line segments.

This page shows Voronoi diagrams generated by my Voronoi code, VRONI. My code is reliable and efficient, and it can cope with any form of polygonal input data, be it clean or not. The design and implementation of VRONI is based on the following four corner stones:

  • Sugihara and Iri's topology-oriented approach;
  • a very careful implementation of the numerical computations required;
  • an automatic relaxation of epsilon thresholds; and
  • a multi-level recovery process combined with desperate mode.
The algorithm was implemented in ANSI C, based on standard floating-point arithmetic. The concept of desperate mode has been borrowed from my triangulation code FIST. To my knowledge, VRONI is the first code for computing Voronoi diagrams of points and line segments which is both reliable and fast. Based on the Voronoi diagram, VRONI can also compute offset patterns or perform buffering. (See my WWW pages on pocket machining for an application of Voronoi-based offsetting.) Since VRONI computes a Voronoi diagram of points as its first stage, the code also supports the reliable computation of point Voronoi diagrams and of Delaunay triangulations. A recent extension can compute a maximum inscribed circle and can extract a (weighted) medial axis transformation (wMAT) from the Voronoi diagram. The code has been tested on polygonal and curvilinear datasets containing up to several hundred thousand points, segments and/or arcs; the largest polygon tested contains 2,124,304 vertices/segments.

As stated above, VRONI is able to compute (families of) offset curves that consist of straight-line segments and circular arcs. Of course, those offset curves may form the basis for a machining tool path. However, assembling those offset curves into a "decent" tool path is left to the user. Depending on what "decent" means this may be trivial or difficult. Since VRONI is used heavily in the CAD/CAM industry, I conclude that it is not horribly difficult to implement a machining algorithm on top of VRONI. In any case, please note that

  • VRONI does not output G-code;
  • VRONI does not generate purely polygonal offsets (except in the interior of a convex shape).

Currently, the handling of circular arcs is restricted to user-defined or VRONI-computed polygonal approximations. Starting with Release 2.0 of VRONI, an approximate Voronoi diagram of points, line segments and circular arcs can be obtained from the true Voronoi diagram of the polygonal approximation of all arcs. The approximation scheme used to approximate circular arcs maintains tangent continuity, at an approximation tolerance set by the user. Also, genuine offset arcs can be generated during offsetting (rather than hundreds of line segments in the standard case of a polygonal approximation of an arc). Similarly, all other VD-based tools and extensions of VRONI (such as the computation of a weighted medial axis) are applicable to the approximate Voronoi diagram of circular arcs.

While this approach to handling Voronoi diagrams of circular arcs is obviously not theoretically correct, it seems to be perfectly fine for practical applications as long as the number of circular arcs is not excessively large. Since segments derived from a polygonal approximation of an arc are handled different than "normal" input segments, and due to the generally high processing speed of VRONI, the Voronoi diagram of several thousands of arcs can be computed at acceptable rates. Likely, the limiting factor when applying VRONI to tons of arcs will be main memory rather than speed. Support for true (rather than approximate) Voronoi diagrams of circular arcs might be added in the near future, but is not yet available.

The source code for VRONI is available for both academic and commercial use. Indeed, the code has been in wide-spread use, both commercially and within academic research projects. Send me an email and I will let you know the details of how you could use my code. However, in order to avoid any disappointment, let me warn you upfront that VRONI has not been released into the public domain!

VRONI has been tested on several thousands of synthetic and real-world data sets. In particular, it was run on hundreds of GIS data sets, with longitude/latitude regarded as standard x,y-coordinates. The following images show Voronoi diagrams which were computed by means of VRONI. (Click on an image icon in order to see the full-size image. The full-size images have 800x800 pixels.) Please note: all visible discontinuities are nothing but graphics artifacts - of course, the Voronoi diagram and the offsets are formed by continuous curves!

Skyline of Salzburg: This data set consists of 1079 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 130 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (You may also want to have a look at some interior offsets computed by VRONI in 30 milliseconds, or at the medial axis.)

Arrangement of line segments: All intersections were found "on the fly". (The input vertices are depicted in the left picture.)

MRI slice of human organs: This data set consists of 1,570 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 160 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Gil Barequet; ftp://ftp.math.tau.ac.il/pub/barequet/psdb/).

Mechanical part: This data set consists of 1,248 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 150 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Markus Thoma.)

Mechanical part: This data set consists of 6,700 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 920 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Taglio S.r.l.)

Offset pattern: This data set was generated by computing inwards and outwards offsets of a (synthetic) star-shaped polygon by means of VRONI. A polygonal approximation of this offset pattern, which consists of 8,608 line segments, was again subjected to VRONI. Computing its Voronoi diagram took 1,220 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30.

Contour lines for tile "ss28_30" of a map of Antarctica: This data set consists of 101,045 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 17,870 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Antarctic Digital Database of the British Antartic Survey.)

Network of the primary roads of India: This data set consists of 159,032 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 27,390 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Digital Chart of the World.)

Hawk Creek - Yellow Medicine River Major Watershed: This data set consists of 61,820 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 9,790 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Minnesota River Basin Data Center.)

Map of the Baltic Sea Region: This data set consists of 89,873 line segments, and computing the Voronoi diagram took 16,120 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (Data courtesy of Baltic Drainage Basin Project.) See also a zoomed view of a central portion of this map ( coastlines, Voronoi diagram).

Biarcs: (Approximate) Voronoi diagram of the biarc approximation of a "random" polygon generated by means of our random polygon generator RPG. This data set consists of 164 circular arcs. We used 13,744 straight-line segments for the temporary approximation of the arcs, and computing the approximate Voronoi diagram took 1,940 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (You may also want to have a look at some interior offsets computed by VRONI; please note that the offsets of the arcs are genuine arcs.)

Random sites: (Approximate) Voronoi diagram of "random" straight-line segments and circles. This data set consists of 353 circular arcs (and several line segments), and a total of 14,269 line segments after approximation of all arcs. Computing the approximate Voronoi diagram took 2,280 milliseconds on a 300MHz Sun Ultra 30. (You may also want to have a look at a family of offsets computed by VRONI; please note that the offsets of the arcs are genuine arcs.)
